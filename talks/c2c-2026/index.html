Every company in this room…

  • …is paying for software that does 80% of what they need and 20% of what they don't.
  • The procurement cycle to replace it: six months.
  • The contract lock in: three years.
  • The UI was designed by a committee in 2009.

And somewhere in that company, someone is keeping a spreadsheet
to work around the tool you're paying $50k a year for.

How to spot unmet software needs

  • Excel doing database work. The universal tell.
  • "We have to manually…" Every dev's ears should perk up.
  • Procurement shaped problems. Pain that's been normalized
    because the alternative seems too expensive.

Build vs. Buy: the honest version

If a $20/month tool already exists and solves your problem,
buy it. Don't write code.

But "build vs. buy" now often means:
hire another company to do $300,000 of custom development work
because the people making the call
don't realize someone in-house could build it for a tenth of that.

"A competent developer can build something better, cheaper, and more tailored to the exact problem in weeks."

Most companies don't even know that's an option.

That's the opportunity.

Value Speaks for Itself

There's a common piece of advice in tech:

"Ask forgiveness, not permission."

I don't love it. It assumes you're doing something that requires forgiveness.

The better frame: build something so good
that forgiveness was never on the table.

Most developers wait.

Enterprise procurement exists to manage risk on uncertain bets.
Your job is to make the bet so obviously good
it collapses that process.

"When you show a director a working tool
that saves her team twenty hours a week,
nobody asks how you spent last Thursday."

Legitimacy comes from outcomes, not process.

Everybody has more time than they think.

The hours in a week where you could be building something high leverage
is much larger than the hours you're currently using that way.

Replacing a tool that costs your company a million dollars a year
is so disproportionate it almost shouldn't be possible.

But it is.

I started as an intern. Nobody told me I was allowed to
build a million dollar replacement for an enterprise tool.

I just looked at the problem, used the tools I had access to, and built it.

YOU CAN JUST DO THINGS.

Flask, Power Apps, a Databricks notebook, whatever.
The tool doesn't matter. The output does.

ACT 5
THE PLAYBOOK

How to find your first win  ·  How to compound it

The one habit that
compounded more than anything.

☕

COFFEE CHATS

One per week  ·  Different team  ·  Just listen.

Finance. Treasury. Ops. Anyone who doesn't write code for a living.
After a month: a heat map of every pain point in the company
and a list of people who already trusted me.

Ask three questions. Every time.

  • 1   "What's the hardest part of your job?"
  • 2   "What tools do you use, and what's frustrating about them?"
  • 3   "What's the most stupidly expensive piece of tech you have to use?"

The snowball question:

"Who are 2–3 people
you think I'd learn something from?"

  • Warm intro. No cold outreach. Instant credibility.
  • After a month: every pain point mapped.
  • People will tell you their real problems if you just ask.

"Complaints are a
product spec
in disguise."

Increasing your luck surface area

Luck surface area = the number of people who know what you can do.

  • Ship fast. One deployed thing beats ten planned ones.
  • Post about what you build internally and publicly. Your SVP is reading.
  • Name wins with numbers. "Saved $40k/month" travels. "Built a dashboard" dies.
  • Make the person you helped your sponsor. They have political capital. They'll spend it on you.

"Hey, I saw your post about that app you built.
There's this thing in my workflow that's annoying.
Could I pick your brain about it?"

That happens every single week once you're consistent.
You stop hunting for ideas. The ideas come to you.

Here's what to do this week.

  1. 01Find one person doing something manually that shouldn't be manual.
  2. 02Ask them the three questions.
  3. 03Sketch the replacement. Don't pitch it yet.
  4. 04Build a slice. Show one person.

Every million dollar tool replacement starts with
one coffee chat and one prototype.

"The companies that win aren't the ones with the biggest software budgets. they're the ones with developers who refuse to accept that the current tool is the only option."

You don't need permission. You need a problem worth solving.
